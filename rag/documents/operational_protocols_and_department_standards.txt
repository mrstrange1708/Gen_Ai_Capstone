CLINIC OPERATIONAL PROTOCOLS FOR APPOINTMENT MANAGEMENT
Source: Hospital Administration and Quality Standards Manual
Version: 2024

SECTION 1: DEPARTMENT-LEVEL NO-SHOW THRESHOLDS

Each clinical department must monitor its rolling 30-day no-show rate 
against established thresholds to determine required intervention intensity.

Threshold Level 1 (no-show rate below 10%):
Standard reminder protocol is sufficient. No additional operational changes 
required. Continue monitoring.

Threshold Level 2 (no-show rate 10-15%):
Department must implement mandatory SMS confirmation for all appointments. 
Patients who do not confirm within 24 hours of the reminder receive a 
follow-up automated call. Analyze top contributing patient-level factors 
to identify whether a specific demographic or risk profile is driving the rate.

Threshold Level 3 (no-show rate above 15%):
Department must implement mandatory SMS confirmation AND conduct a formal 
operational review within 30 days. Permissible to introduce controlled 
overbooking at 10-15% in slots historically known for high no-show rates 
(e.g., Friday afternoons, early Monday mornings). Overbooking must not 
exceed 15% and must be applied only to documented high-no-show slots — 
blanket overbooking is an unacceptable protocol that degrades patient 
experience and creates resource bottlenecks.

SECTION 2: HIGH-RISK PATIENT MANAGEMENT WORKFLOW

When a patient is algorithmically flagged as HIGH RISK for no-show 
(probability score above 0.70), the following workflow must be executed:

Step 1 — Immediate confirmation dispatch:
Send automated SMS confirmation request within 1 hour of the flag being 
raised. Message must include a one-tap confirm/reschedule option.

Step 2 — Care coordinator assignment:
Assign a dedicated care coordinator for a 5-minute pre-visit preparation 
call. This call serves three purposes: logistical confirmation (does the 
patient know where to go, how to get there), barrier identification 
(is there anything making attendance difficult), and motivational engagement 
(reinforcing the clinical importance of the visit).

Step 3 — Day-before check:
If no confirmation has been received 24 hours before the appointment, 
the care coordinator makes a direct phone call. If the patient is unreachable, 
the slot is placed on a priority waitlist for a waitlisted patient.

Step 4 — Post-no-show protocol:
If the patient does not attend, do not simply reschedule. Initiate a 
barrier identification outreach within 48 hours to understand why attendance 
failed. Use this data to update the patient's risk profile and care plan.

SECTION 3: SCHEDULING BEST PRACTICES

Monday morning slots (7:00-9:00 AM) and Friday afternoon slots (3:00-6:00 PM) 
consistently show the highest no-show rates across departments. Where 
operationally possible, reserve these slots for low-risk patients or same-day 
bookings from the waitlist.

Same-day appointment slots have the lowest no-show rates of any scheduling 
type, often below 5%. An active waitlist system should be maintained to 
fill cancellations and gaps with same-day patients who are ready and available. 
This maximizes clinical capacity without increasing total appointment supply.

Patients who have a documented pattern of cancelling and rescheduling 
multiple times must be required to speak directly with triage before 
any further rebooking is accepted. This conversation serves to identify 
underlying barriers and assess whether a different appointment format 
(teleconsultation, home visit, group session) might better serve the patient.

SECTION 4: TELECONSULTATION AS INTERVENTION

Teleconsultation is not a lesser alternative to in-person care — it is 
a clinically appropriate and evidence-supported mode of delivery for a 
defined set of appointment types. Using teleconsultation proactively for 
high-risk patients who face genuine attendance barriers is a patient-centered 
intervention, not a compromise.

Appointment types suitable for teleconsultation intervention:
- Medication review and prescription renewal
- Post-procedure follow-up (non-wound-related)
- Mental health counseling and therapy
- Chronic disease management check-ins
- Lab result discussion
- Specialist referral initial consultations

Appointment types that require in-person attendance:
- Physical examination and diagnostic procedures
- Surgical consultations requiring physical assessment
- Imaging and diagnostic testing
- Procedures and interventions

When offering teleconsultation to a high-risk patient, frame it as a 
service upgrade, not a downgrade. Communication should emphasize convenience 
and accessibility, not the patient's risk profile.

SECTION 5: ETHICAL STANDARDS IN RISK-BASED INTERVENTION

Algorithmic risk scores must be used as decision-support tools, not 
deterministic gatekeeping mechanisms. A high risk score indicates a 
patient who needs additional support — it does not indicate a patient 
who is less deserving of care.

Interventions must be designed to remove barriers, not penalize patients 
for predicted behavior. Overbooking a patient's slot, deprioritizing their 
scheduling, or reducing their care quality on the basis of a predicted 
no-show constitutes an ethical violation and undermines the purpose of 
the system.

All risk-based interventions must be documented in the patient record 
with clear rationale. Patients have the right to be informed that 
predictive tools are being used in their care coordination and to 
opt out of algorithmic classification if they choose.

Care coordinators must be trained to use risk scores as conversation 
starters, not conversation conclusions. The score opens the door to 
understanding barriers — it does not close the door on patient agency.
