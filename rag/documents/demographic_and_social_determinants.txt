SOCIAL DETERMINANTS OF HEALTH AND APPOINTMENT ATTENDANCE
Source: Patient Engagement and Equity Framework
Version: 2024

SECTION 1: TRANSPORTATION AND DISTANCE

Geographic distance between a patient's residence and the clinic is a 
well-established predictor of appointment non-attendance. Patients traveling 
more than 15km show meaningfully higher no-show rates than those within a 
5km radius, with the effect compounding significantly beyond 30km.

For patients with a travel distance exceeding 15km, teleconsultation should 
be evaluated as an appropriate alternative where the clinical nature of the 
appointment permits. This is particularly relevant for follow-up consultations, 
medication reviews, and non-invasive assessments.

Patients with a travel burden exceeding 45 minutes of estimated travel time 
should be prioritized for block scheduling — consolidating multiple required 
appointments into a single visit day — to minimize the total number of trips 
required. This intervention is particularly impactful for patients managing 
chronic conditions requiring multi-department visits.

During adverse weather conditions such as heavy rainfall or extreme heat, 
patients traveling long distances face disproportionate barriers. On days 
where severe weather is forecast, proactively contact high-distance patients 
to offer teleconsultation switching or appointment rescheduling. Waiting for 
the patient to cancel is the wrong protocol; proactive outreach preserves 
both patient experience and slot utilization.

SECTION 2: EMPLOYMENT STATUS

Unemployed patients have higher aggregate no-show rates, but the causal 
mechanism is different from what is commonly assumed. The primary drivers 
are not lack of time (as might be assumed with unemployed individuals) but 
rather increased psychological stress, financial anxiety, and reduced 
perceived health prioritization.

For unemployed patients, offer flexible scheduling across morning, afternoon, 
and evening slots. Evening appointment slots show higher attendance rates 
specifically for blue-collar employed patients who cannot take daytime leave, 
while unemployed patients generally do not have a strong time-of-day 
preference.

Where subsidized transportation assistance is available, proactively inform 
unemployed patients with long travel distances at the time of booking rather 
than waiting for them to request it. Passive availability of support services 
does not reach the patients who need them.

SECTION 3: CHRONIC CONDITIONS AND MULTI-MORBIDITY

Patients managing multiple chronic conditions — particularly combinations 
such as diabetes and hypertension, or COPD and heart disease — face higher 
cognitive and logistical load in managing their healthcare. This increased 
burden is associated with higher appointment non-compliance.

For patients with two or more chronic conditions, evaluate eligibility for 
transportation assistance or remote monitoring programs as part of the 
standard care plan, not as a reactive measure after a missed appointment.

These patients should also be considered for care navigator assignment — 
a single point of contact who coordinates all appointment scheduling, 
reminders, and logistical support. Fragmented communication across multiple 
departments is itself a driver of missed appointments in multi-morbid patients.

SECTION 4: AGE-SPECIFIC PROTOCOLS

Pediatric appointments (patients under 18):
Attendance depends entirely on parent or guardian availability and awareness. 
Reminders must be directed to the registered guardian, not the patient. 
Reminder messages should include calendar integration links (Google Calendar, 
iCal) to accommodate working parents managing complex schedules.

Young adults (18-30):
This demographic has the second-highest no-show rate after elderly patients. 
Key characteristics: low phone answer rates, high SMS engagement, tendency 
to deprioritize preventive or non-urgent appointments. Reminders should be 
brief, action-oriented, and contain a frictionless confirmation mechanism. 
Avoid long-form email communications for this group.

Middle-aged adults (31-64):
Generally the most reliable demographic for attendance. Standard reminder 
protocols are sufficient. For employed patients in this group, Monday morning 
and Friday afternoon slots should be avoided where possible as these have 
the highest no-show rates due to work schedule pressures.

Elderly patients (65+):
Highest-risk demographic from a communication standpoint. Many elderly 
patients do not use smartphones reliably and may have visual or cognitive 
impairments that reduce effectiveness of written reminders. Protocols:
- Prioritize voice call outreach
- Register and maintain a caregiver or family contact
- Provide clear wayfinding instructions (parking, entrance, floor) in all 
  reminder communications, as navigation confusion is a documented contributor 
  to elderly patient no-shows

SECTION 5: LANGUAGE AND LITERACY BARRIERS

Patients for whom the clinic's default communication language is not their 
primary language face a compounding barrier: they may not fully understand 
appointment details even when reminders are received. This results in 
no-shows that are recorded as patient non-compliance but are actually 
system failures.

All appointment reminders should be sent in the patient's registered 
primary language where translation infrastructure is available. Where it 
is not, reminders should at minimum include the date, time, and location 
in a visually clear, non-text-heavy format.

First-time patients at any clinic have higher no-show rates than returning 
patients, partly due to unfamiliarity with the facility. For new patients, 
include wayfinding details, parking instructions, arrival time expectations, 
and what documents to bring in the booking confirmation. This single 
intervention measurably reduces first-visit no-shows.
